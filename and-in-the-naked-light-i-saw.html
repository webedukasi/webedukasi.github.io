6. What is the difference between glare and light clutter (light pollution)?​


Light pollution, also known as photopollution, is the presence of anthropogenic light in the night environment. It is exacerbated by excessive, misdirected or obtrusive uses of light, but even carefully used light fundamentally alters natural conditions. As a major side-effect of urbanization, it is blamed for compromising health, disrupting ecosystems and spoiling aesthetic environments.

13. what is the difference between Light as a wave and Light as an Energy? and please explain it in english.


Jawaban:

"Light as a wave" and "Light as energy" are two different ways of understanding and describing the nature of light. Let's explore each concept:

1. Light as a wave:

In the wave model of light, light is considered to be an electromagnetic wave. This theory, proposed by James Clerk Maxwell in the 19th century, describes light as a form of electromagnetic radiation. According to this model, light consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that propagate through space.

Key characteristics of light as a wave include:

- Wave properties: Light exhibits properties such as wavelength (λ), frequency (f), and amplitude (A). Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive peaks or troughs of the wave, frequency is the number of wave cycles passing a point in a second, and amplitude represents the intensity or brightness of the light.

- Interference and diffraction: Light waves can interfere with each other, leading to constructive or destructive interference patterns. Additionally, light waves can diffract around obstacles, bending and spreading out.

- Polarization: Light waves can be polarized, meaning the oscillations of the electric and magnetic fields are restricted to specific orientations.

2. Light as energy:

When we say "light as energy," we are referring to the idea that light is composed of photons, which are discrete packets or quanta of electromagnetic energy. This concept is explained by the quantum theory of light, which originated from the work of Albert Einstein and Max Planck in the early 20th century.

Key characteristics of light as energy (photons) include:

- Photons: Light can be thought of as a stream of tiny particles called photons. Each photon carries a specific amount of energy that depends on its frequency. Higher-frequency photons have more energy than lower-frequency photons.

- Particle-like behavior: Photons can exhibit particle-like behavior, such as being absorbed or emitted by atoms or other particles in discrete interactions.

- Photoelectric effect: The photoelectric effect, first explained by Einstein, demonstrates that when light (composed of photons) shines on certain materials, it can cause the emission of electrons from the material's surface.

In summary, "light as a wave" describes light using the wave model of electromagnetic radiation, focusing on its wave-like properties, while "light as energy" refers to the concept of light being composed of discrete packets of energy called photons, as explained by the quantum theory of light. Both models provide valuable insights into the nature of light and are used in different contexts depending on the phenomena being studied.

39. An aurora is a natural light display in the sky particularly in the latitude (Artic and Antartica) regions, caused by the collision of energetic charged particles with atoms in the high latitude atmosphere (thermosphere). The charged particles originate in thr magnestosphere and solar wind, and are directed by the Earth's magnetic field into atmosphere. Most aurora occur in a band known as the auroral zone which is typically 3◦ to 6◦ in latitudinal extent and at all local times or longitudes. Aurora is classified as diffuse and discrete aurora. The diffuse aurora is a featureless glow in the sky which may not be visible to the naked eye in a dark night and defines the extent of the auroral zone. Discrete auroras are sharply defined features within the diffuse auroras wich vary in brightness from just barely visible to the naked eye to bright enough to read newspaper at night sky because they are not as bright as the sunlit sky.
